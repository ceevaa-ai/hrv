Findings

What the Numbers Say

1
The median (37 ms) is your real baseline, not the mean (41.5 ms)
Breathe sessions and outlier spikes pull the average up by ~11%. For honest daily tracking, use the median.
2
Single-day readings are unreliable — daily swings average ~21 ms
A 30 ms swing in 24 hours is normal. The signal only emerges across 7–14 day windows. Never react to one bad reading.
3
2025 wasn't just higher — it was the most consistent good year
Highest mean and lowest relative volatility (CV = 50.5%). Not occasional spikes — sustained performance. Jan–Feb 2025 had 8 straight weeks above 40 ms.
4
Acute shocks don't crash your HRV. Extended transitions do.
Surgery → 44 ms. Company closure → 44 ms. But 5 months of building and adjusting → 28.7 ms and falling. The body handles discrete events better than open-ended stretches.
5
March 2026 vs March 2024: same month, −48%
Mar 2024 averaged 55.6 ms. Mar 2026: 28.7 ms. Includes a recent low of 9.9 ms on Mar 7. Three years of progress, currently reversed.

Sustained lows — 14-day avg below 35 ms
1
Apr – Jun 2023 · ~10 weeks · avg 22–34 ms
The deepest trough in the dataset. Apr 29 – May 28 averaged 22.9 ms — the worst 30-day window ever. 9 consecutive readings below 30 ms. Early adaptation period.
2
Nov 2023 – Feb 2024 · ~13 weeks · avg 32–35 ms
26 consecutive weeks below lifetime mean — the longest sub-mean streak. Not crisis-level, but persistently suppressed through winter. The long grind.
3
Late Aug – Oct 2023 · ~5 weeks · avg 30–34 ms
Post-surgery tail. The acute surgery held at 44 ms, but the weeks-long recovery was harder than the surgery itself.
4
Feb – Mar 2026 · 7+ weeks (ongoing) · avg 29–34 ms
Currently active. 7 consecutive readings below 30 ms (Feb 5–12). Second-longest sustained low after Apr–Jun 2023. Hasn't stabilised yet.
The takeaway: 2025 had three distinct high periods totalling ~17 weeks above 48 ms. The body has had 4 low periods, two of which (2023) eventually recovered into those highs. Track record of coming back: 2 for 2.

Story

The Bottom Line

A body that climbed from 37.9 ms to 46.8 ms — through surgery, a marriage, a layoff, a company launch — knows something about resilience. The question is whether it remembers.

Over three years, the data shows a clear upward arc: improving HRV, falling resting heart rate, growing resilience to acute stressors. Jan–Feb 2025 — right after the layoff, right at the company launch — were the two best months in the dataset. Purpose mattered more than stability.

What the data also shows: a 5-month decline from Nov 2025 (44.9 ms) to Mar 2026 (28.7 ms) that is the longest sustained downward trend recorded. Unlike surgery or company closure — which the body absorbed — this extended building-and-adjusting phase is registering differently. The body stays in a mild alert state, unable to fully downregulate.

The birthday arc (26.8 → 32.2 → 50.0 ms) proves the trajectory is real. The 2025 data proves the capacity exists. The location data adds another dimension: Bengaluru at 43.7 ms is measurably better than Prayagraj (37.2) or Bhubaneswar (37.5) — whether it's the partner, the routine, or the city itself, the nervous system has a clear preference. The current dip is a setback, not an erasure. The data is a roadmap back — summer activity, consistent sleep, purposeful work, breathing practice, and maybe being in the right city. The tools are already proven.

What this data can tell you
General autonomic trends over time · Recovery relative to your baseline · Signals of stress, illness, or poor sleep · Response to behaviour changes over weeks · Correlation (not causation) between life events and health
What it can't
Diagnose any condition · Distinguish stress vs illness vs poor sleep · Predict future outcomes · Replace clinical ECG measurement · Establish causation · Account for reading type (overnight vs Breathe session)
Methodology note
This dataset mixes passive overnight and active Breathe session readings. Spikes above ~85 ms are likely session readings, not resting state. Apple Watch uses SDNN via PPG, not clinical rMSSD via ECG. Trends are meaningful; absolute numbers are approximate. Not medical advice.
